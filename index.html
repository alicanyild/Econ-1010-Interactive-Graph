The model behind the diagram

Everything here is built on straight-line (linear) demand and supply, so every result has a clean closed form you can check by hand.

Demand & supply

Inverse demand is \(P = a - bQ\) and inverse supply is \(P = c + dQ\). The intercepts \(a\) and \(c\) set each curve's position; the slopes \(b\) and \(d\) set steepness — a bigger slope means a steeper, more inelastic curve. The wide slider ranges let you push toward nearly flat (very elastic) or nearly vertical (very inelastic).

Equilibrium & surplus

Setting demand equal to supply gives \(Q^{*} = \dfrac{a-c}{b+d}\) and \(P^{*} = a - bQ^{*}\). The triangle between demand and \(P^{*}\) is consumer surplus; the triangle between \(P^{*}\) and supply is producer surplus.

Elasticity

Measured at the equilibrium point: \(\lvert\varepsilon_D\rvert = \dfrac{1}{b}\cdot\dfrac{P^{*}}{Q^{*}}\) and \(\varepsilon_S = \dfrac{1}{d}\cdot\dfrac{P^{*}}{Q^{*}}\). These determine who bears a tax or captures a subsidy.

Taxes

A per-unit tax \(t\) opens a wedge: consumers pay \(P_c\), producers keep \(P_c - t\). The consumer's share of the burden is \(\dfrac{b}{b+d} = \dfrac{\varepsilon_S}{\varepsilon_S + \lvert\varepsilon_D\rvert}\) — the more inelastic side pays more. Revenue is \(t\cdot Q\) and deadweight loss is \(\dfrac{1}{2}\cdot\dfrac{t^{2}}{b+d}\).

Subsidies

A per-unit subsidy \(s\) is the mirror image of a tax, pushing output above the efficient level. Its benefit lands by the same incidence rule a tax burden does — the more inelastic (steeper) side captures the larger share, \(\dfrac{b}{b+d}\) going to consumers and \(\dfrac{d}{b+d}\) to producers (\(=\dfrac{\varepsilon_S}{\varepsilon_S + \lvert\varepsilon_D\rvert}\)). Government spending of \(s\cdot Q\) is a transfer to the market, while the over-production still wastes \(\dfrac{1}{2}\cdot\dfrac{s^{2}}{b+d}\) as deadweight loss.

Externalities

An external effect of \(e\) per unit shifts the relevant private curve to a social one — MSC for a production effect, MSB for a consumption effect. The efficient quantity is where \(\text{MSB} = \text{MSC}\); the gap from the market quantity creates \(\dfrac{1}{2}\cdot\dfrac{e^{2}}{b+d}\) of deadweight loss, which a Pigouvian tax or subsidy of \(e\) would remove.

Price controls

A ceiling \(\bar P\) binds only when \(\bar P < P^{*}\); a floor \(\underline P\) binds only when \(\underline P > P^{*}\). At the controlled price the quantities are \(Q_d = \dfrac{a-P}{b}\) and \(Q_s = \dfrac{P-c}{d}\); a ceiling leaves a shortage \(Q_d - Q_s\) and a floor a surplus \(Q_s - Q_d\). The short side rations the market, so trade is \(Q_t = \min(Q_d, Q_s)\).

Because the price no longer clears the market, a wedge opens at \(Q_t\) between what the marginal buyer would pay, \(D(Q_t)\), and what the marginal seller would accept, \(S(Q_t)\) — and nobody collects it. Under a ceiling the good is cheap but scarce, so buyers compete for it by queueing, searching and waiting; under a floor buyers are scarce, so sellers compete for them with idle capacity and wasteful quality. Either way the rectangle \([\,D(Q_t) - S(Q_t)\,]\cdot Q_t\) is dissipated: it is the total value of the wasted resources, not a transfer. What is left is the consumer-surplus triangle above \(D(Q_t)\), equal to \(\tfrac{1}{2}bQ_t^{2}\), and the producer-surplus triangle below \(S(Q_t)\), equal to \(\tfrac{1}{2}dQ_t^{2}\).

On top of that, the units between \(Q_t\) and \(Q^{*}\) are never traded at all — the familiar lost gains from trade, \(\tfrac{1}{2}(Q^{*}-Q_t)\,[\,D(Q_t)-S(Q_t)\,]\). The accounting closes exactly: \(CS + PS + \text{wasted resources} + DWL = \) free-market total surplus. (This is the full-dissipation benchmark. If buyers waste less than the whole wedge — say the queue is short, or a black market re-sells the good — part of the rectangle survives as a transfer instead of vanishing.)

Shifts versus movements along a curve

A shifter changes how much is demanded or supplied at every price, moving the whole curve. A change in the good's own price is different — it just slides you along a fixed curve.

Demand shifters

Anything that changes quantity demanded at a given price: tastes and preferences, income (up shifts a normal good right, but shifts an inferior good left), the number of buyers, the price of a substitute (its rise pushes this good's demand right) or a complement (its rise pushes this good's demand left), and expected future prices (expecting a rise pulls buying forward, so buyers stockpile now). A rightward shift is an increase in demand.

Supply shifters

Technology, input costs, producer taxes and subsidies, entry or exit of firms, sellers' price expectations (expecting a rise makes them hold output back today), and opportunity cost — if another crop such as wheat becomes more profitable, a farm moves land away from soybeans, cutting soybean supply. A rightward shift is an increase in supply.

When one curve moves

If only demand shifts, the equilibrium slides along the fixed supply curve: demand up raises both price and quantity; demand down lowers both. If only supply shifts, it slides along demand: supply up lowers price and raises quantity; supply down raises price and lowers quantity. Both price and quantity are pinned down.

When both curves move

With demand and supply shifting together, only one of price or quantity is determinate and the other depends on which shift is larger. Demand up with supply up raises quantity but leaves price ambiguous; demand up with supply down raises price but leaves quantity ambiguous — with the mirror results for decreases.
